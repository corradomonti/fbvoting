What is this?

Liquid FM is a recommendation system for music in which you decide who's good at recommending music. Usually such a system tries to guess your tastes from what your friends listen to. In Liquid FM, instead, you can entrust someone else to be a good recommender. The more trust someone gets, the more important their suggestions are. Anybody can entrust other people, in turn, carrying on part of their importance. And you can recommend what music to listen to, as you are always in some way important.

This chain of trust — that flows from you to the people you nominated — translates, mathematically, to a hype score computed specifically for every person. Every song is then ranked according to the score of all the people who voted for it. This score in the end results in the global recommendations you see.

Personal recommendations are computed in a very similar way, but weighting more the delegates you have personally voted for. This results in a higher consideration of their own delegates (and so on) and — obviously — of the song they chose.

What is viscous democracy?

Liquid FM is the first application for music recommendation adopting the approach of viscous democracy. This is in fact the first time one tries to apply this kind of semi-direct democracy in a real scenario. Viscous democracy was described in a research paper and it is a variant of liquid (or delegative) democracy.

Pure liquid democracy is thought as a compromise between direct democracy (where the citizens directly express their opinions on each matter) and representative democracy (where citizens elect representatives that take decisions on their behalf). In a liquid system, each citizen may choose whether to express his/her opinion or to delegate somebody to do that. Liquid democracy has been attracting more and more attention lately, and it is being used actively in a variety of contexts. Nonetheless, its indiscriminate usage in a social network is problematic: as our vote travels farther away through a delegation chain, we would like to introduce some reluctance in the way the power is transferred to other people we may not know directly. For this reason, viscous democracy includes a damping factor (like Katz's index or PageRank do) to reduce the amount of power delegated through long chains. Technically, our system of viscous democracy is a system of transitive proxy voting with exponential damping.

This system (fully described in the above cited paper) is used to establish a per-genre guru score, that is then used to determine the music recommendations you see; full details on how this is done will be provided in a forthcoming paper.
